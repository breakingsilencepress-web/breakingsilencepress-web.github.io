A Secrecy System Hiding Inventions

For decades, U.S. national security policy has enabled the suppression of civilian inventions — blocking patents from publication, prohibiting foreign filing, and erasing technologies from the public record. This is not an exception. It is a system.

The Evidence

Hundreds of U.S. patents have been placed under secrecy orders using the Invention Secrecy Act of 1951, often renewed indefinitely under claims of national security — without public oversight or transparency.

Blocked from Publication

Inventions are withheld from the public record, sometimes for decades, preventing disclosure, peer review, and civilian use.

No Foreign Filing

Inventors are legally prohibited from filing or discussing their work outside the United States.

Complete Secrecy

In some cases, even the existence of an invention is classified, creating permanent informational blackouts.

Breaking Silence documents how this secrecy regime was built, how it operates, and how thousands of inventions quietly disappeared inside it — using declassified policy, government statistics, and international secrecy laws.

About The Book

Breaking Silence investigates how transformative technological knowledge can be suppressed under the authority of national security—often without public oversight or transparency.

Beginning with a real U.S. patent placed under secrecy order, the book expands outward to expose a broader system built through Cold War doctrine, federal law, and classified operational policy.

Drawing on declassified government documents, legal analysis, and historical case studies, it traces how secrecy orders operate in practice and why their justifications often remain classified—even from those directly affected.

Timeline of Secrecy

2007

Patent Application Filed

Budimir and Desanka Damnjanović file U.S. Patent Application No. 11/881,492 with the USPTO for a dual-use infrared missile countermeasure intended to protect aircraft from heat-seeking missiles.

2009

Secrecy Order Imposed

After national security review under the Invention Secrecy Act (35 U.S.C. § 181), the USPTO places the application under a secrecy order at the request of the U.S. Air Force.

2012

Administrative Compensation Claim Filed

The U.S. government places a secrecy order on the patent, preventing its publication under the Invention Secrecy Act. The technology is deemed critical to national security.

2013

Secrecy Order Renewed

The U.S. Air Force renews the secrecy order, extending restrictions on disclosure and commercialization without providing a public explanation or declassification rationale.

2015

Patent Issued

The inventors file suit in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Michigan, alleging statutory and constitutional violations and seeking just compensation.

2017

Case Concluded Confidentially

he litigation concludes through a confidential settlement and dismissal, with no public disclosure of damages or release of secrecy-order justifications.

Present

No Commercial Deployment

Despite declassification, the patent has not been commercially deployed, and the prolonged suppression coincided with rapid advances in missile-defense technology.
